# Phase 0 ACT-015B QP-Style Constrained Update Report

## Summary

- Goal: test whether M7c's contact-preserving heuristic can be formalized as a constrained update that stays close to contact descent while bounding auxiliary harm.
- Method: add `M8a/M8b` QP-grid approximations. They enumerate existing cone directions, filter by contact descent and per-auxiliary tolerance, then choose the projected update closest to the contact-only update.
- Main result: mixed. `M8a` gives the strongest soft terminal candidate by a large margin, but does not beat M3 on top-k BT PAE/contact and has worse update harm. `M8b` improves update safety but fails on candidate quality.
- Best positive signal: `M8a` soft terminal has BT PAE 7.0264 and BT ipTM 0.5880 versus M3 soft BT PAE 8.7660 and BT ipTM 0.5183. Under BT ipTM reranking at budget 8, `M8a` top-k reaches BT ipTM 0.4800 versus M3 0.4576.
- Blocking result: under the main BT PAE rerank at budget 8, M3 remains best with BT PAE 9.3614; `M8a` reaches 11.3780, `M7c` 12.1912, and `M8b` 12.9887.
- Decision: ACT-015B is promising enough to tune, but not ready to scale. The next experiment should constrain `M8a`'s auxiliary harm more effectively without losing its strong soft-terminal geometry.

## 1. Experiment Motivation

ACT-015A showed that naive CEM collapses around poor hard candidates. That result pushed the project back toward gradient geometry rather than pure hard-sample selection.

ACT-015B asks whether the core M7c idea can be made cleaner:

> Instead of heuristically sorting cone candidates, can we solve a small constrained update problem: stay close to the contact direction, require contact descent, and cap auxiliary harm?

This directly matches the project motivation: Mosaic can combine several oracles, but the optimizer should use their geometry in a more controlled way than scalar averaging.

## 3. Core Algorithm or Method

The intended constrained update is:

```text
minimize_d ||d - d_contact||^2
subject to g_contact^T d <= -epsilon
           g_i^T d <= tau_i for each auxiliary oracle
```

The implemented prototype is a candidate-set approximation:

1. build the same cone candidate set used by M7c;
2. compute each candidate's actual projected simplex update;
3. compute the projected contact-only update;
4. keep candidates that preserve a minimum contact descent ratio and keep every auxiliary derivative below `aux_slack`;
5. choose the feasible candidate closest to the contact-only projected update;
6. if infeasible, choose the candidate with smallest auxiliary violation.

Variants:

| Method | Aux tolerance | Contact descent ratio |
|---|---:|---:|
| `M8a` | 0.08 | 0.60 |
| `M8b` | 0.04 | 0.60 |

## 6. How to Read the Tables

The update-level table says whether the optimizer obeys the intended local geometry. The candidate tables say whether that local geometry becomes usable discrete sequences.

The key distinction is `M8a` versus `M8b`:

- `M8a`: best soft candidate and best top-k BT ipTM, but too much auxiliary harm and weaker BT PAE/contact than M3.
- `M8b`: better harm control, but its terminal candidates are not good enough.

This tradeoff is exactly why ACT015B should be tuned rather than accepted or killed immediately.

## 7. Interpretation

ACT015B answers the immediate question:

> Can QP-style constrained geometry recover the soft candidate quality that naive CEM could not?

Partial answer: yes, for relaxed soft candidates. `M8a` is the strongest soft-terminal method seen in this run, with BT PAE 7.0264 and BT ipTM 0.5880.

But it does not yet answer the paper-facing optimization question, because M3 remains best under BT PAE/contact top-k handoff and `M8a` has higher update harm. The current QP-grid objective is too contact-aggressive: it moves closer to contact-only descent, improves interface confidence, but allows too much auxiliary damage and does not reliably produce the best PAE/contact selected hard candidates.

## 8. Conclusion and Discussion

### What was the goal?

The goal was to replace M7c's heuristic direction choice with a more explicit constrained multi-objective update.

### Did we get the answer?

Yes. The result is mixed but informative: QP-style constrained updates are more promising than naive CEM, but the current `M8a/M8b` settings do not pass the full reduced gate.

### How did we find it?

We implemented `contact_qp_grid`, ran it against M3 and M7c on Quest H100, then analyzed update-level directional derivatives and best-of-1/4/8 candidate handoff under BT PAE, BT ipTM, and contact reranking.

### Are the results correct?

The run completed with exit status 0. The comparison uses the same seeds, target, length, candidate sampling temperature, top-k budget, Protenix cache, and reranking script. The conclusion is internally consistent: soft metrics favor M8a, BT ipTM top-k favors M8a, but BT PAE/contact and update-harm gates still favor M3 or M8b.

## 9. Limitations and Caveats

- Reduced ProtenixMini setup only: 2 seeds, 24-residue binder, target length 48.
- The QP is approximated by grid search over candidate directions, not solved continuously.
- `M8a/M8b` differ only in auxiliary tolerance; no sweep over contact ratio or fallback ranking was run.
- The top-k candidate pool is small: 8 samples per seed per method.
- This does not test cross-oracle holdout or final binder-design filter pass rates.

## 10. Next Steps

1. Tune ACT015B before moving to ACT015C: add a small sweep over auxiliary slack and contact descent ratio.
2. Try a middle variant between `M8a` and `M8b`, likely `aux_slack=0.06`, plus a less aggressive contact ratio such as `0.40`.
3. Add a feasibility/violation diagnostic to each step so we know whether candidates are truly satisfying QP constraints or mostly using fallback.
4. Keep M3 top-k as the main candidate baseline.
5. Do not scale ACT015B until a variant beats M3 on BT PAE or contact without exceeding M3 update harm.
